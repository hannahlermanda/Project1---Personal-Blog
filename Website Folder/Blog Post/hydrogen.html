Hydrogen

Published on: April 5, 2023 by John Doe


We are the representatives of the cosmos; we are an example of what hydrogen atoms can do, given 15 billion years of cosmic evolution.
- Carl Sagan, American Astronomer

Fast Facts

Atomic Number: 1

Chemical Group Block: Nonmetal

Standard State: Gas

Year Discovered: 1766

History

Hydrogen was discovered in 1766 by British scientist Henry Cavendish. He conducted a chemical experiment in which zinc metal reacted with hydrochloric acid to produce zinc chloride and hydrogen. The formula for this reaction can be seen below.

It wasn't until 1786 that hydrogen got its name that we know it as, coined by French chemist Antoine Lavoisier. It was named hydrogen through its Greek meaning (hydro- meaning "water" and -gen meaning "origin").

Uses

Hydrogen is one of the most abundant elements on Earth. It makes up less than 1% of the Earth's crust, but it is part of ALL of the ocean. Alone, it may not actively do or seem like much, as it is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas. Being extremely reactive, it has the potential to participate in many chemical reactions and is used for a variety of purposes today.

Electricity

When hydrogen is combined with oxygen and put into a fuel cell, it is capable of producing electicity, water, and heat. Unlike other sources of energy, hydrogen powered fuel cells do not emit much carbon dioxide and other toxic chemicals into the air, making it an environmental friendly source of energy that scientists are looking into. It is being explored as an energy source in vehicles and back-up energy in vital buildings like hospitals.

Rocket Fuel

Liquid hydrogen, in combination with liquid oxygen, has been utilized to fuel rockets and satellites. As a fuel, it has the greatest propulsion with the least amount of material used, making it a very suitable fuel for traveling far. Due to hydrogen's cryogenic property (the need for a gas to be at an extremely low temperature in order to convert to a gas), liquid hydrogen must be stored at -423 degrees °F (approximately -253 degrees °C) to keep it from evaporating before use. A rocket's or space object's fuel tank must be able to keep heat out of the tank, but be able to expand to accomodate for the element's rapid expansion when it heats up as the fuel is used. An improper tank will either lead to the hydogen disappearing or the tank exploding.
